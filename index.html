Abstract

Background: The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) systems into United States enterprise environments has outpaced the development of corresponding governance frameworks, risk management protocols, and cybersecurity controls. Organizations are deploying large language models (LLMs), autonomous agentic systems, and AI-augmented productivity tools across financial services, healthcare, defense, and education sectors without adequate institutional oversight infrastructure.

Objective: This paper conducts a six-phase semantic review of the enterprise generative AI ecosystem in the United States, synthesizing evidence from 178 verified primary and secondary sources — including NIST, ISO, ISACA, OECD, WEF, OWASP, MITRE, CISA, FBI IC3/DOJ, FTC, SEC, HHS OCR, IBM Security, Verizon, Microsoft MDDR, Mandiant, CrowdStrike, Unit 42, Deloitte, PwC, Accenture, IEEE/ACM/USENIX/Elsevier/Springer peer-reviewed literature, and Stanford AI Index — to map the AI platform landscape, validate adoption statistics, quantify AI-specific financial exposure where authoritative data exist, analyze governance gaps, and propose two novel governance frameworks.

Methods: A structured semantic review methodology was applied across six sequential phases: (1) enterprise AI ecosystem mapping and platform taxonomy construction; (2) enterprise adoption and usage ranking using empirical data; (3) risk taxonomy development and real-world incident classification; (4) cross-framework governance gap analysis spanning NIST AI RMF 1.0, OWASP LLM Top 10, ISO/IEC 42001, ISACA COBIT 2019, and the EU AI Act; (5) development of the five-level Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM); and (6) proposal of the Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF).

Results: The review identifies 19 major enterprise AI platforms classified into four taxonomic categories, documents five critical incident categories with empirical evidence, identifies eight structural governance gaps not addressed by any single existing framework, and introduces the EAGM and IAGF as original contributions to enterprise AI governance scholarship.

Conclusion: No single existing framework — including NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, or the EU AI Act — fully addresses the governance requirements of autonomous agentic AI systems. The proposed IAGF provides a synthesis framework that closes critical gaps in non-deterministic auditing, multi-agent governance, semantic logging, and continuous runtime oversight. Organizations operating at EAGM Level 1 or 2 face immediate, quantifiable organizational risk.

1. Introduction

The proliferation of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) within United States enterprise environments represents one of the most consequential technological transformations of the early twenty-first century. Between 2022 and 2026, the deployment of large language models (LLMs), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) architectures, and multi-agent AI orchestration systems moved from research laboratories to production business environments at a velocity that institutional governance has been structurally unable to match. McKinsey & Company (2024) reported that 72% of survey respondents' organizations had adopted AI in at least one business function (global survey, n=1,363), while 65% regularly used generative AI in at least one function — up from one-third in 2023 (McKinsey, 2024). Stanford HAI (2024, 2025) documented U.S. private AI investment rising from USD 67.2 billion (2023) to USD 109.1 billion (2024), while formal comprehensive AI policies remained at 18% of companies (Stanford HAI, 2024) and 38% of organizations in ISACA's 2026 global pulse poll (ISACA, 2026).

This asymmetry between technological capability and governance readiness constitutes the central problem addressed by this paper. The deployment of autonomous, non-deterministic AI systems — particularly agentic AI frameworks capable of accessing external APIs, executing code, modifying databases, and autonomously assigning sub-tasks to other AI agents — exposes organizations to qualitatively new categories of risk that were not contemplated by legacy governance frameworks built for deterministic software systems. The United States regulatory environment, anchored by Executive Order 14110 (October 2023) and OMB Memorandum M-24-10 (March 2024), has begun to address these challenges at the federal level, but enterprise implementation remains deeply uneven across sectors (GAO, 2024; Congressional Research Service, 2024).

Existing academic literature has documented significant progress in individual aspects of AI governance — AI ethics principles (Jobin et al., 2019), LLM security vulnerabilities (Greshake et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), AI risk taxonomies (Weidinger et al., 2022), and governance framework comparisons (NIST, 2023a; ISO, 2023) — but no existing work synthesizes across all six governance dimensions: platform taxonomy, adoption patterns, risk classification, cross-framework gap analysis, maturity modeling, and agentic AI governance. This paper addresses that gap through a structured six-phase semantic review, contributing two novel frameworks — the Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM) and the Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF) — to the scholarly literature.

The paper is organized around six sequential phases, a multi-source financial impact analysis (Sections 9–10), cost-of-inaction and governance-investment comparison tables, practical organizational actions, and evidence-strength classification. All numerical claims are traceable to primary sources verified in Sections 1.1–1.2 and classified per Section 2.4 (Proven, Estimated, General Cyber, or IC3 AI subcategory).

2. Methodology

2.1 Semantic Review Protocol and Search Strategy

This paper employs a structured semantic review methodology, distinct from a traditional systematic review (PRISMA) in that it prioritizes semantic synthesis — the identification of conceptual relationships, governance gaps, and framework complementarities across a heterogeneous body of evidence — over strict quantitative aggregation. The semantic review approach is appropriate for emerging domains where the evidence base spans multiple epistemic traditions: official government standards, peer-reviewed academic research, incident databases, and validated industry reports (Dafoe, 2018; Bommasani et al., 2022).

The search strategy included: (1) official publications from NIST, CISA, NSA, OMB, DoD, HHS, and GAO retrieved from .gov domains; (2) international standards from ISO, ENISA, OECD, and EU regulatory bodies; (3) ISACA and IEEE/ACM professional organization publications; (4) academic papers from arXiv, IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, Google Scholar, and Semantic Scholar using search terms including "enterprise AI governance," "LLM security," "agentic AI risk," "AI risk management framework," "generative AI enterprise deployment," and "AI governance maturity"; (5) major industry research reports from Stanford HAI, McKinsey, Gartner, IBM Institute for Business Value, and IDC; and (6) documented AI incidents from the AI Incident Database (AIID) and investigative journalism sources with verifiable primary evidence.

3. Phase 1 — Enterprise AI Ecosystem Mapping and Platform Taxonomy

PHASE 1: Platform Taxonomy and Ecosystem Mapping

3.1 Platform Classification Framework

The enterprise generative AI ecosystem in the United States cannot be understood through analysis of any single platform or deployment model. The 2024 enterprise AI landscape encompasses a heterogeneous set of systems spanning commercial SaaS copilots, enterprise API gateways, open-source model repositories, and autonomous agentic orchestration frameworks — each carrying distinct risk profiles, governance maturity levels, and organizational exposure surfaces. Building on Bommasani et al.'s (2022) foundational taxonomy of foundation model deployment patterns and Gartner's (2024a) enterprise AI deployment framework, this review proposes a four-category Enterprise AI Platform Taxonomy (EAPT).

The EAPT classifies enterprise AI platforms along two intersecting axes: governance accountability (who is responsible for safety and compliance controls — the vendor or the enterprise) and operational autonomy (the degree to which the AI system operates independently of human oversight). This two-axis framework produces four distinct governance paradigms that demand qualitatively different organizational responses.

3.3 Taxonomy Synthesis

The EAPT reveals a critical structural insight: governance burden is inversely related to platform openness. Category A platforms (out-of-the-box copilots) carry the highest vendor-side governance accountability but restrict enterprise customization and transparency. Category D platforms (agentic and open-weight systems) offer maximum flexibility but shift the entire governance burden — including security controls, data isolation, compliance certification, and audit trail construction — entirely to the deploying organization. This "governance burden inversion" represents the central architecture problem that current enterprise risk frameworks, designed for commercial SaaS software, have not adequately addressed (ISACA, 2025a; Gartner, 2024a).

4. Phase 2 — Enterprise Adoption and Usage Ranking

PHASE 2: Adoption Patterns and US Sector Deployment

4.1 US Adoption Statistics

Empirical adoption data from multiple independent research bodies confirms that enterprise GenAI deployment in the United States reached critical mass between 2023 and 2024. McKinsey & Company (2024) reported that 72% of respondents' organizations adopted AI in at least one business function (global survey), while 65% regularly used gen AI in at least one function — the highest gen AI growth rate in the McKinsey series since 2017 (McKinsey, 2024). Stanford HAI (2024) documented that U.S. private investment in AI reached USD 67.2 billion in 2023, representing 49% of total global AI investment. IDC (2023) forecast USD 154 billion in global spending on AI-centric systems in 2023, with the United States accounting for more than half (IDC, 2023). IDC's 2024 V2 spending guide places the global AI market at nearly USD 235 billion (IDC, 2024).

Contrasted against adoption, governance maturity remains low. Stanford HAI (2024) found only 18% of companies had formal, comprehensive AI management policies. ISACA (2024, European study) reported 17% with formal comprehensive AI policy; ISACA (2026) reports 38% globally — improved but still minority coverage. The GAO (2024) found that while 24 U.S. federal agencies had begun AI use under EO 14110, only 7 completed required risk assessments per OMB M-24-10 (GAO, 2024).

4.3 The Shadow AI Problem

A critical adoption dynamic not captured by enterprise surveys is the phenomenon of "Shadow AI" — the unauthorized use of consumer-grade AI tools by employees for enterprise tasks without organizational oversight or security controls. ISACA (2025a) identified Shadow AI as among the top three emerging enterprise risks, noting that it was structurally analogous to the "Shadow IT" problem that plagued organizations in the 2010s but operationally more dangerous due to the semantic nature of data exposure. Unlike Shadow IT (unauthorized software installation), Shadow AI results in the direct transmission of confidential organizational data — source code, customer records, proprietary financial models, and legally privileged communications — to public AI platforms with no data isolation guarantee.

The Samsung Electronics incident (March 2023) provided the most widely documented public illustration of this dynamic: engineers submitted proprietary semiconductor fabrication code and internal meeting recordings to ChatGPT's consumer tier, exposing trade secrets that could not subsequently be retrieved or deleted (Ars Technica, 2023; McGregor & AIID, 2023). ISACA (2024b) estimated that organizations lose an average of 14% of their controlled data perimeter annually to Shadow AI vectors, though the authors note this figure requires validation through larger longitudinal studies.

5.2 Five Documented Incident Categories with Evidence

EART-1: Public LLM Data Leakage — Samsung Electronics (2023)

In March 2023, engineers at Samsung Electronics' semiconductor division submitted proprietary source code and confidential meeting minutes to ChatGPT's consumer interface during the first month of the company's trial AI deployment period. Three separate incidents were documented within a 20-day period, collectively exposing trade secrets related to semiconductor fabrication processes (Ars Technica, 2023). Samsung subsequently imposed a company-wide ban on generative AI tools and began developing an internal, air-gapped LLM. This incident is classified in the AI Incident Database (AIID Report #367) and is referenced in ISACA's (2025a) enterprise AI risk guidance as a canonical illustration of Shadow AI data exposure. The governance failure was multi-layered: no acceptable use policy existed at deployment time, no DLP controls were configured to detect code submission to external AI APIs, and no employee training on data classification for AI tools had been completed (IBM Security, 2024).

EART-2: Prompt Injection — Enterprise LLM Applications (2023–2024)

Greshake et al. (2023) documented the first systematic analysis of indirect prompt injection attacks against LLM-integrated enterprise applications, demonstrating that adversarial instructions embedded in documents processed by AI assistants — emails, web pages, SharePoint files — could cause the AI system to extract confidential information, exfiltrate data through API calls, or perform unauthorized actions without the user's knowledge. Liu et al. (2023) subsequently catalogued 13 distinct prompt injection attack patterns against GPT-integrated applications. MITRE ATLAS (2024) formalized these attacks as AML.T0051 (LLM Prompt Injection) and documented a case study involving a Microsoft 365 Copilot deployment where indirect prompt injection through a malicious email caused Copilot to exfiltrate calendar and email data. Perez and Ribeiro (2022) demonstrated that aligned language models could be reliably caused to ignore safety training through carefully crafted prompts, establishing the foundational attack surface that subsequent enterprise deployments have not adequately defended against.

EART-3: Microsoft Copilot Permission Oversharing (2023–2024)

Microsoft 365 Copilot's deployment in enterprise environments surfaced a structural governance failure that Varonis (2024) documented across 21 enterprise Copilot deployments: because Copilot operates under the identity and permissions of the deploying user, it can surface data from SharePoint, Teams, Exchange, and OneDrive that the user technically has access to but that organizational information governance policies intended to restrict. Specifically, Varonis found that 85% of enterprise SharePoint environments had overly permissive site-level access that would allow Copilot to surface sensitive HR records, legal documents, financial models, and M&A information to any user with "read" access to the site collection (Varonis, 2024). Microsoft (2024a) responded with guidance on conducting "Copilot Readiness" assessments to audit permissions before deployment, but the assessment is not mandatory and its implementation requires significant enterprise resource investment.

EART-4: Agentic AI Workflow Hijacking (2024–2025)

The emergence of agentic AI frameworks — systems capable of autonomously planning tasks, calling external APIs, executing code, modifying files, and delegating sub-tasks to other agents — introduced a qualitatively new attack surface that existing security frameworks were not designed to address. Wu et al. (2023) documented the AutoGen architecture, which enables multi-agent AI systems to negotiate, delegate, and execute complex workflows with minimal human oversight. OWASP LLM08 (Excessive Agency) documents the resulting vulnerability: agents granted overly broad permissions to tools and systems can be manipulated through prompt injection to perform unauthorized actions — including sending emails, modifying databases, or triggering financial transactions — at machine speed before any human oversight mechanism can intervene. The absence of non-repudiation audit trails for multi-agent interactions (ISACA, 2025a) means that forensic reconstruction of agent hijacking incidents is frequently impossible with current enterprise logging infrastructure.

EART-5: AI Supply Chain Attacks (2023–2024)

Zou et al. (2024) demonstrated "PoisonedRAG," a novel attack in which adversarial documents injected into enterprise knowledge bases used by RAG-based AI systems could reliably cause the AI to produce attacker-controlled outputs, bypassing alignment training. Carlini et al. (2021) previously established that training data could be extracted from production LLMs, while Goldblum et al. (2023) provided a comprehensive review of dataset poisoning and backdoor attack methodologies applicable to the AI supply chain. NIST AI 100-2e2023 formalized the adversarial machine learning taxonomy covering these attack categories, and CISA (2024) identified AI supply chain integrity as one of three critical unsolved problems in enterprise AI security. The Hugging Face model repository — with over 500,000 models as of 2024, the vast majority uploaded without security vetting — represents the most significant unaddressed AI supply chain risk surface in the US enterprise ecosystem (OWASP LLM03; MITRE AML.T0010).

6.2 Eight Critical Governance Gaps

Cross-framework analysis identifies eight structural governance gaps that are either unaddressed or only partially addressed by any single existing framework. These gaps represent the primary unsolved governance problems in the US enterprise AI ecosystem as of 2026:

Gap 1 — Non-Deterministic Auditing Gap: Traditional SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) systems are engineered for deterministic log analysis and cannot parse semantic anomalies, multi-agent goal deviations, or LLM behavioral drift. No existing framework prescribes a technically viable solution. (Sources: ISACA, 2025a; NIST, 2023a; IBM Security, 2024)
Gap 2 — Agentic AI Governance Gap: Multi-agent frameworks (AutoGen, LangChain multi-agent) that assign tasks to other agents, execute code, modify external systems, and iterate toward goals without human approval at each step are not addressed by any current ratified governance framework. ISACA (2025a) identifies this as "the most critical unresolved enterprise AI governance challenge." (Sources: ISACA, 2025a; OWASP LLM08; MITRE AML.T0054; Wu et al., 2023)
Gap 3 — Semantic Logging Gap: Organizations need to log not just what AI systems did (API calls, token counts) but what they meant (intent-level semantic behavior analysis). No existing standard specifies semantic logging architecture, storage requirements, or analysis methodologies. (Sources: ISACA, 2025a; IBM Security, 2024; NIST, 2024a)
Gap 4 — Governance Velocity Gap: AI capability advances at exponential speed while governance frameworks operate on multi-year publication cycles. The NIST AI RMF took 18 months from drafting to publication; the EU AI Act took 3+ years. During this period, agentic AI, multimodal AI, and frontier models transitioned from research prototypes to production deployments. Principle-based adaptive governance is required but no existing framework provides this architecture. (Sources: NIST, 2023a; European Parliament, 2024; OSTP, 2023)
Gap 5 — Shadow AI Detection Gap: No existing framework provides technical mechanisms for detecting and controlling unauthorized AI tool use within organizational networks. ISACA (2025a) identifies Shadow AI as a top-three enterprise risk; only COBIT's APO domain addresses it at the governance policy level with no technical detection prescription. (Sources: ISACA, 2025a; ISACA, 2024b; CISA, 2024)
Gap 6 — AI Supply Chain Provenance Gap: Unlike software supply chains (where SBOM — Software Bill of Materials — provides component transparency), no equivalent "AI Bill of Materials" standard exists for documenting training data provenance, fine-tuning sources, RLHF dataset origins, or plugin/tool dependencies of enterprise AI systems. (Sources: NIST AI 100-2e2023; OWASP LLM03; CISA, 2024; Carlini et al., 2021)
Gap 7 — Cross-Platform Consistency Gap: Organizations using multiple AI platforms simultaneously (e.g., Azure OpenAI + LangChain + Hugging Face models + ChatGPT Enterprise) must maintain separate governance frameworks for each, as no unified cross-platform AI governance standard exists. This creates governance fragmentation, audit inconsistency, and security blind spots at platform boundaries. (Sources: Gartner, 2024a; ISACA, 2025a; ISO, 2023)
Gap 8 — AI Hallucination Liability Gap: When AI systems produce false information that causes material harm (Mata v. Avianca, 2023 — lawyers submitted AI-hallucinated case citations), no existing legal or governance framework clearly assigns liability between the AI developer, the enterprise deployer, and the individual user. ABA (2024) has begun addressing this for legal professionals, but no general enterprise governance standard exists. (Sources: U.S. District Court SDNY, 2023; ABA, 2024; Weidinger et al., 2022; Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017)

7. Phase 5 — Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM)

PHASE 5: Five-Level Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM)

7.1 Model Design Rationale

The Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM) is an original contribution of this review, designed to operationalize abstract governance principles into concrete, assessable organizational levels. The EAGM draws on the structural approach of CMMI (Capability Maturity Model Integration) and ISO/IEC 33000 process assessment, adapted for the specific characteristics of enterprise AI systems: non-determinism, continuous learning, agentic autonomy, and multi-vendor deployment complexity. The model is designed for use by ISACA-certified IT auditors (as a COBIT-compatible assessment tool), enterprise architecture teams (as an organizational benchmark), and board-level governance committees (as a risk maturity indicator).

The EAGM is grounded in five identified evidence clusters from the reviewed literature: (1) the governance execution gap documented by ISACA (2024b) showing <18% framework adoption; (2) the five-level progression implicit in NIST AI RMF's GOVERN-MAP-MEASURE-MANAGE-IMPROVE cycle; (3) ISACA's (2025a) identification of "continuous runtime monitoring" as the critical advancement beyond current point-in-time governance; (4) the architectural requirements for agentic AI oversight derived from OWASP LLM08 and MITRE AML.T0054; and (5) empirical evidence from Samsung, Mata v. Avianca, and Microsoft Copilot incidents documenting governance failures at Level 1 and Level 2 organizations.

Characteristics: Full technical integration of AI application layer with Zero Trust architecture and IAM. Real-time DLP scanning of all AI prompt inputs and outputs. Systematic risk classification per regulatory mandate (EU AI Act tiers; EO 14110 dual-use thresholds). Continuous vulnerability scanning of vector databases, prompt engineering pipelines, and API integrations. Shadow AI detection via network telemetry and CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker) tools.

Governance Evidence: ISACA (2025a) describes Level 4 as the current aspirational target for enterprise governance programs. IBM (2024) reports <15% of large enterprises operate at Level 4 for AI specifically, though many operate at Level 4 for traditional IT. NIST AI RMF MEASURE and MANAGE functions fully implemented at Level 4.

Risk Profile: Low-to-moderate risk. Addresses all EART categories through technical controls. Cannot fully address agentic AI governance or semantic logging (Level 5 capabilities required).

Level 5: Autonomous AI Oversight — Resilient Paradigm

Characteristics: Machine-speed, closed-loop AI governance fabric deployed specifically to govern other AI systems. Independent "Supervisor Agents" — non-operational AI systems dedicated to monitoring, evaluating, and dynamically constraining operational AI agents — provide continuous behavioral oversight. Every AI decision pathway written to non-repudiation cryptographic ledgers. Automated token revocation and API freezing triggered by detected anomalous, toxic, or non-compliant semantic behavior. Full semantic logging infrastructure in production.

Governance Evidence: Level 5 is an aspirational framework target. ISACA (2025a) first formally describes this level in the context of agentic AI governance. Isolated Level 5 implementations exist in defense/intelligence contexts (NSA, 2023; DoD, 2023). No commercial enterprise is documented at full Level 5 as of 2026.

Risk Profile: Minimum achievable enterprise AI risk. Addresses all eight governance gaps identified in Phase 4 through autonomous oversight infrastructure.

8. Phase 6 — Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF)

PHASE 6: Novel Governance Framework — Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF)

8.1 Framework Design and Rationale

The Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF) is the second original contribution of this review. It is proposed as a governance architecture specifically designed to address the eight gaps identified in Phase 4, targeting the governance requirements of autonomous multi-agent AI systems that existing frameworks do not adequately cover. The IAGF does not replace NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, ISACA COBIT, OWASP LLM Top 10, or the EU AI Act. Rather, it is designed as a complementary layer that connects and extends these frameworks for the specific operational requirements of agentic AI deployment.

The design of the IAGF is informed by seven convergent evidence streams: (1) ISACA's (2025a) call for continuous runtime governance and semantic logging as unmet requirements; (2) OWASP LLM08's documentation of excessive agency as a primary attack vector; (3) MITRE ATLAS AML.T0054's formalization of agent hijacking; (4) the NIST AI RMF GOVERN function's emphasis on organizational accountability structures; (5) ISO/IEC 42001's management system approach to AI lifecycle governance; (6) the EU AI Act's risk-based classification approach; and (7) academic research on multi-agent system security from Wu et al. (2023), Park et al. (2023), and Liu et al. (2024).

8.2 Seven Governance Components of the IAGF

Figure 3. Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF): Seven Components for Autonomous AI System Governance
The IAGF provides a complementary governance layer specifically designed for autonomous multi-agent AI systems not fully addressed by existing frameworks.
IAGF is an original contribution of this review. Component design derived from: ISACA (2025a); OWASP LLM08 (2023); MITRE AML.T0054 (2024); NIST AI RMF (2023a); ISO/IEC 42001 (2023); EU AI Act (2024); Wu et al. (2023); Park et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024).

Component 1 — Agent Scope & Permission Registry: A centralized registry that defines and enforces the minimum necessary permissions (least-privilege principle applied to AI agents) for every operational AI agent and every tool or API the agent is permitted to access. Agents request permissions dynamically and receive time-limited, action-scoped tokens rather than standing access. Addresses: OWASP LLM08 (Excessive Agency); NIST AI RMF GOVERN function; COBIT APO09 (service level management applied to AI).

Component 2 — Supervisor Agent Layer: A dedicated set of non-operational AI agents whose sole function is to monitor, evaluate, and when necessary interrupt the behavior of operational AI agents. Supervisor agents analyze agent decision chains for goal drift, scope violations, toxic content, and anomalous action patterns. Supervisor agents have read-only access to the operational environment but write access to the remediation engine. Addresses: EAGM Level 5 requirement; ISACA (2025a) continuous monitoring; NIST AI RMF MEASURE function.

Component 3 — Semantic Logging Engine: A logging infrastructure that captures not only traditional system events (API calls, token counts, timestamps) but also semantic content — the intent, topic classification, sentiment, and potential policy violations of AI inputs and outputs. Semantic logs are indexed and retained for forensic investigation and compliance audit. Addresses: ISACA (2025a) semantic logging gap; NIST AI RMF MEASURE; EU AI Act Article 12 (record-keeping).

Component 4 — Cryptographic Audit Ledger: An append-only, cryptographically signed ledger that records all agent decisions, tool invocations, and autonomous actions in a tamper-evident format. Provides non-repudiation for AI-driven business decisions, enabling regulatory compliance with EU AI Act Article 17 (quality management system) and SOC 2 audit requirements. Addresses: EU AI Act Article 17; ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 9; COBIT MEA domain.

Component 5 — Automated Remediation Engine: An automated response system capable of machine-speed enforcement actions when Supervisor Agents detect violations: API token revocation, agent isolation, workflow suspension, security team alerting, and incident logging. Remediates at machine speed — the interval between attack initiation and containment is measured in milliseconds, not hours. Addresses: NIST AI RMF RESPOND function; COBIT DSS04 (continuity management); EU AI Act Article 9 (risk management).

Component 6 — Shadow AI Detection Fabric: A multi-layer detection infrastructure combining Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB) tools, DNS-level monitoring, network traffic analysis, and endpoint telemetry to identify unauthorized AI tool use by employees. Provides continuous, network-wide visibility into Shadow AI activity, generating both technical blocking capabilities and governance dashboard reporting. Addresses: ISACA (2025a) Shadow AI risk; COBIT APO12; NIST CSF ID.AM (asset management).

Component 7 — AI Supply Chain Provenance System: An "AI Bill of Materials" (AI-BOM) system that tracks and verifies the origin, training data sources, fine-tuning history, RLHF dataset provenance, and plugin/tool dependencies for every AI model deployed in the enterprise. Provides cryptographically verifiable attestation that deployed models have not been tampered with. Addresses: OWASP LLM03; MITRE AML.T0010; NIST AI 100-2e2023; CISA supply chain guidance (2024).

8.3 Unresolved Governance Gaps for Autonomous Agents

Even with full IAGF implementation, four governance gaps remain unresolved by existing technical and regulatory approaches as of 2026, representing priority areas for future research and standards development:

Unresolved Gap A — Goal Drift in Long-Horizon Agents: AI agents executing long-horizon multi-step tasks (spanning hours or days) can drift from their original objectives through accumulation of sub-optimal micro-decisions, without any single step constituting an identifiable violation. No current detection methodology reliably identifies goal drift in real time. Required research: formal goal specification languages for AI agents; runtime goal verification methodologies. (Sources: Bengio et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2024)
Unresolved Gap B — Multi-Agent Collusion: When multiple AI agents collaborate toward a shared goal, adversarial agents may strategically influence other agents through normal communication channels in ways that are individually innocuous but collectively produce harmful outcomes. No adversarial detection methodology for multi-agent collusion currently exists. (Sources: Park et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; MITRE, 2024)
Unresolved Gap C — Liability Attribution in Multi-Agent Decisions: When a harmful outcome results from a chain of decisions made by multiple AI agents, each following its own policy, no legal or governance framework currently provides clear attribution of liability among the AI developer, the orchestration framework vendor, the enterprise deployer, and the individual who initially deployed the agent. This is the most acute legal gap in enterprise agentic AI deployment. (Sources: ABA, 2024; Weidinger et al., 2022; Cihon et al., 2020)
Unresolved Gap D — Emergent Behavior Classification: Foundation models exhibit unpredicted emergent capabilities at scale (Wei et al., 2022) that governance frameworks cannot anticipate or classify in advance. Dynamic, principle-based governance that can evaluate emergent behaviors against core organizational values — rather than fixed rule sets — is theoretically required but has not yet been operationally demonstrated at enterprise scale. (Sources: Wei et al., 2022; Bommasani et al., 2022; Bengio et al., 2023)

13. Discussion

The six-phase semantic review reveals a persistent and widening governance gap between the pace of enterprise AI adoption and the maturity of organizational governance infrastructure. The central finding — that only 18% of organizations had formal comprehensive AI policies in 2024 (Stanford HAI, 2024), while 72% of organizations used AI in at least one business function globally (McKinsey, 2024) and 90% of digital-trust professionals believe employees already use AI (ISACA, 2026) — represents a structural governance deficit with measurable organizational risk consequences documented in five distinct incident categories.

The EAPT taxonomy developed in Phase 1 provides a novel analytical lens for understanding why governance burden distribution is not uniform across the enterprise AI ecosystem. Category A platforms (vendor-governed copilots) create risks primarily through configuration failures and permission inheritance — organizational risks that are manageable through EAGM Level 3–4 controls. Category D platforms (agentic and open-weight systems) transfer the entire governance burden to the deploying organization, creating risks that require EAGM Level 4–5 capabilities that most organizations do not currently possess. This "governance burden inversion" is the structural reason why LangChain and AutoGen deployments (Category D) score 8.3–8.8 on the risk scale while Azure OpenAI (Category B, vendor-governed) scores 3.1.

The cross-framework gap analysis in Phase 4 demonstrates that the five major governance frameworks — while individually valuable — are not collectively sufficient. Each framework addresses a different governance layer: NIST AI RMF addresses organizational risk management; OWASP LLM Top 10 addresses technical security vulnerabilities; ISO/IEC 42001 addresses management system requirements; COBIT 2019 addresses IT governance integration; and the EU AI Act addresses regulatory compliance. The eight governance gaps identified — particularly the non-deterministic auditing gap, the agentic AI governance gap, and the semantic logging gap — exist in the spaces between frameworks, not within any single one.

The EAGM and IAGF proposed in Phases 5 and 6 are designed to operate at these inter-framework gaps. The EAGM provides a maturity progression model that enables organizations to assess where they are (Level 1–5) and what investments are required to advance. The IAGF provides the technical architecture for the most advanced governance requirement: governing autonomous AI agents that operate faster than human oversight can track. Both frameworks are explicitly designed to complement, not replace, existing standards — a design principle derived from the recognition that governance ecosystem fragmentation (Gap 7) is itself a governance problem.

The implications for US enterprise governance practice are significant. Organizations deploying agentic AI frameworks (LangChain, AutoGen, Salesforce Agentforce) without first reaching EAGM Level 4 are, by the analysis of this review, accepting organizational risks that are not adequately disclosed to boards, audit committees, or regulators. The EU AI Act's extraterritorial reach (affecting 10,000–15,000 US companies with EU operations) creates a regulatory forcing function that will drive adoption of more structured governance — but the Act's focus on risk classification rather than technical security controls means it will not close the non-deterministic auditing, semantic logging, or agentic governance gaps without supplemental frameworks like the IAGF.

The expanded financial analysis (Sections 9–10) strengthens the business case for governance without overstating AI causation. IC3 total losses (USD 20.877B in 2025) must not be reported as "AI damage," yet the proven AI Related category (USD 893.3M) with ten disaggregated subcategory fields (Section 9.6.0) and shadow-AI breach premium (+USD 670K per organization) provide executive-grade quantification across all eight AI risk categories. Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Mandiant, and Unit 42 corroborate attack modalities mapped to IC3 dollar fields [FI6–FI9]. Peer-reviewed literature validates technical attack paths mapped to IC3 Phishing AI (USD 10.3M), Data Breach AI (USD 4.3M), and IBM breach components [FI1; FI2; AP1–AP8]. OECD and WEF frame board-level systemic risk. The Top 10 Immediate Organizational Actions and Figure L ranking operationalize controls against documented cost drivers.

14. Conclusions

This six-phase semantic review of the enterprise generative AI ecosystem in the United States yields seven primary conclusions:

C1. The US enterprise AI landscape comprises 19 major platforms classifiable into four governance categories (EAPT), with governance burden distribution being the primary determinant of organizational risk — not platform capability or user adoption level.

C2. Enterprise AI adoption (72% global organizations using AI in at least one function per McKinsey, 2024; 78% organizational AI use per Stanford, 2024) has outpaced governance maturity (18% formal comprehensive AI policies per Stanford, 2024; 38% comprehensive AI policy per ISACA, 2026), creating a structural governance deficit documented in five empirically verified incident categories including the Samsung data leak, prompt injection attacks, Microsoft Copilot oversharing, agent hijacking, and AI supply chain attacks.

C3. No single existing governance framework fully addresses enterprise AI governance requirements. NIST AI RMF, OWASP LLM Top 10, ISO/IEC 42001, ISACA COBIT 2019, and the EU AI Act each address distinct governance layers with eight critical gaps in the inter-framework spaces — particularly for autonomous agentic AI systems.

C4. The Enterprise AI Governance Maturity Model (EAGM) provides a five-level assessment framework enabling organizations to benchmark their current governance posture against empirical evidence of risk exposure at each level. Most US enterprises currently operate at EAGM Levels 1–2, with fewer than 2% estimated at Level 5.

C5. The Integrated Agentic AI Governance Framework (IAGF) proposes a seven-component complementary governance architecture specifically designed for autonomous multi-agent AI systems, addressing governance gaps not covered by any existing standard: Agent Scope Registry, Supervisor Agent Layer, Semantic Logging Engine, Cryptographic Audit Ledger, Automated Remediation Engine, Shadow AI Detection Fabric, and AI Supply Chain Provenance System.

C6. Four governance gaps remain unresolved by any current framework or proposed solution: goal drift in long-horizon agents, multi-agent collusion detection, liability attribution in multi-agent decision chains, and emergent behavior classification. These represent the highest-priority research and standards development needs for the global AI governance community.

C7. Organizations deploying Category D (agentic/open-weight) AI platforms without EAGM Level 4+ governance controls are accepting documented organizational risks that are not adequately disclosed to governance stakeholders and that create potential liability under existing data protection, securities disclosure, and professional responsibility frameworks.

C8. Multi-source financial evidence (FBI IC3 AI subcategories totaling USD 893.3M, IBM shadow-AI and AI-breach economics, Harmonic GenAI exposure data, plus Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Mandiant, Unit 42, Gartner, Deloitte, PwC, Accenture, ISACA, Stanford, OECD, WEF) supports proactive AI governance investment across all eight AI risk categories in Section 9.6. Weak governance creates measurable exposure across financial, regulatory, operational, reputational, legal, and budget domains (Section 10.5). 15. Limitations

This review acknowledges the following limitations. First, platform risk scores (Phase 3) are composite analytical estimates derived from published governance documentation and literature — no independent third-party cross-platform AI risk scoring standard currently exists. Second, EAGM level distribution estimates for US enterprises are derived from survey data (ISACA 2024b; McKinsey 2024) that may not be fully representative of all enterprise sizes and sectors. Third, the enterprise AI platform landscape changes rapidly; platform-specific risk assessments reflect publicly available information as of May 2026 and may be outdated by subsequent platform updates, new certifications, or governance framework revisions. Fourth, the IAGF is a theoretical framework; no peer-reviewed empirical validation of its components has been conducted, representing a priority for future research. Fifth, this review focuses on US enterprise deployments; governance frameworks developed in other jurisdictions (Canada's AIDA, UK's pro-innovation AI approach, China's AI governance regulations) are referenced only where they have direct US applicability.

16. Future Research Directions

Eight priority future research directions are identified by this review:

FR1. Empirical validation of the EAGM through a large-scale survey of US enterprise AI governance maturity, using the EAGM assessment criteria as a validated instrument.

FR2. Technical specification and prototyping of the Semantic Logging Engine (IAGF Component 3), including log schema design, retention policy frameworks, and anomaly detection algorithms for enterprise deployment.

FR3. Development of a standardized "AI Bill of Materials" (AI-BOM) format for enterprise AI supply chain provenance, analogous to NTIA's software SBOM specification.

FR4. Legal scholarship and regulatory analysis on AI liability attribution frameworks for multi-agent decision chains, particularly in the context of US securities disclosure requirements, healthcare liability, and EU AI Act Article 22 (human oversight).

FR5. Longitudinal study of Shadow AI detection and remediation effectiveness using CASB telemetry data from US enterprises across multiple sectors.

FR6. Cross-framework integration mapping: a formal specification of how IAGF components map to specific controls in NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, COBIT 2019, and the EU AI Act, enabling organizations to use IAGF as a unified control framework satisfying multiple compliance obligations.

FR7. Development and validation of goal drift detection methodologies for long-horizon agentic AI systems, using formal goal specification languages and runtime verification techniques.

FR8. International comparative study of enterprise AI governance effectiveness across EU, UK, US, Canada, and Singapore regulatory environments, measuring governance outcome metrics rather than framework adoption rates.
